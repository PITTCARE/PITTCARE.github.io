A new term for the field

What is Intelligence-Technology?

We use Intelligence-Technology (IT) as a unified term for the application of machine learning, clinical decision support, and data-driven systems in health care — broader and more enduring than "AI" alone.

The term "Artificial Intelligence" is useful shorthand, but it understates the full scope of what is transforming primary care — and it will age. Intelligence-Technology captures the continuum: from simple decision-support algorithms to large language models, from risk stratification to automated documentation, from wearable sensors to ambient AI. It also names the tension our center exists to navigate: intelligence that must serve human values, and technology that must earn its place in the exam room.

The name

Every letter is a founding principle

Each letter in PITTCARE encodes a value drawn from the canonical language of family medicine, as defined by the AAFP and ABFM. Letters marked with ◆ carry a Voice of the People dimension — a commitment to hearing patients and communities as active participants, not passive subjects.

P
Primary Care

Anchored in the Department of Family Medicine at the University of Pittsburgh — the continuous, front-line home for individuals, families, and communities across all ages and conditions.

I
Intelligence-Technology

A unified term for the application of machine learning, clinical decision support, and data-driven systems in health care — broader and more enduring than "AI" alone.

T
Translational

Research reaches people only when it is built with them. Translation means co-producing knowledge alongside the patients, families, and clinicians who will live with its consequences — the Voice of the People Survey is how we ensure that direction never drifts from those it is meant to serve.

T
Transformation

Driving measurable change in how care is delivered, how clinicians are supported, and how communities experience the benefits — and bear the risks — of Intelligence-Technology in primary care.

C
Comprehensive

Care that addresses the full scope of a person's health — across conditions, life stages, and the biopsychosocial dimensions that define whole-person, relationship-centered medicine.

A
Accessible

Accessible means more than reach — it means a seat at the table. Patients and communities should have a meaningful voice in how Intelligence-Technology is designed, governed, and deployed in their care. The Voice of the People Survey is our annual commitment to that principle.

R
Relationship-Centered

The physician-patient bond is one relationship; the center's relationship with the public is another. Through the Voice of the People Survey, we extend the relational model of family medicine outward — listening to patients, families, and clinicians each year, not just treating or studying them.

E
Everyone

Optimal health for everyone requires that everyone be heard. The Voice of the People Survey is our structural commitment to that — reaching a representative cross-section of patients, families, and physicians annually, so that no community's experience of Intelligence-Technology goes unrecorded or unaddressed.

A hallmark commitment

Voice of the
People Survey

Because Intelligence-Technology in health care changes rapidly, PITTCARE conducts a structured-interview study every year — reaching patients, families, and physicians — to understand what people think about the use of Intelligence-Technology in their care.

The survey is not a one-time snapshot. It is a standing relationship between the center and the public — an annual commitment to listening before acting, to co-producing knowledge with the people most affected by the technologies we study.

Results are published each year and disseminated through clinical, academic, community, and media channels — ensuring that the perspectives of everyone, not just those already in the room, continuously shape the center's research agenda.

Why annual?

Intelligence-Technology in health care evolves faster than most research cycles. What patients trust or fear today may shift as new tools enter their care. Annual listening captures that movement — and holds the center accountable to it.

Who is heard?

A representative cross-section — patients across ages, backgrounds, and conditions; family physicians across practice settings. Structured interviews, not surveys, so that context and nuance are captured alongside responses.

How results are shared

Published as an open-access annual report and disseminated through clinical, community, academic, and media channels — designed to reach the people it represents, not just the researchers who conduct it.
