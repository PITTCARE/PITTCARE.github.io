Dr. Kravchenko is the primary research faculty of PITTCARE. Her work focuses on the practical application of artificial intelligence and machine learning in family medicine and primary care — with a consistent emphasis on translation: ensuring that methods developed in research reach real clinicians and real patients.

Her research spans three interconnected areas. First, developing practical AI/ML and statistical models that can be translated into clinical settings — particularly in the family medicine and primary care domains. Second, creating analytical tools that inform clinical decision support by leveraging electronic health records to extract patient-specific information and contextualize clinical alerts. Third, translational research using large-scale EHR datasets including the NCATS-funded National COVID Cohort Collaborative (N3C), the NIH-funded All of Us program, and the ENACT Network.

Her most recent interest is AI ethics — exploring data responsibility, algorithmic fairness, the misuse of technology, safe integration of AI into clinical practice, long-term monitoring, and accountability. This thread connects directly to PITTCARE's Voice of the People Survey: the question of who gets to shape how Intelligence-Technology enters the exam room.

Dr. Kravchenko holds a PhD in Chemistry from the University of British Columbia and completed an NLM postdoctoral fellowship at the Pitt Department of Biomedical Informatics — the same department that provides PITTCARE's foundational informatics partnership through Dr. Visweswaran.

Active project · ENACT Network
Concierge Clinical
Evidence Service (CCES)
A clinician-focused pilot service for answering real-world clinical questions with real-world EHR data
Submit a question ↗

Uses de-identified, aggregate EHR data from up to 50 participating health systems — giving answers grounded in real patient populations, not just published trial cohorts.

Supports questions where existing evidence or clinical guidelines are limited or unclear — the gap between what guidelines say and what the patient in front of you looks like.

Returns an interpretable summary to help inform clinical decision-making — written for clinicians, not data scientists, with explicit acknowledgment of limitations.

Free to clinicians and supported by grant funding — no cost, no contract, no informatics expertise required to submit a question.

How it works

01
Submit your question

Go to the CCES website and complete a short form with your contact information and clinical question.

02
Refine the evidence

The CCES team follows up to clarify your question and translates it into an EHR query across the ENACT network.

03
Analyze the data

The query runs against de-identified, aggregate data from up to 50 participating health systems. No informatics expertise required.

04
Receive a concise response

You receive an interpretable summary — real-world evidence to inform your thinking and your conversation with the patient.

Questions CCES can answer

Best suited for questions that can be defined using diagnoses, medications, procedures, laboratory results, encounters, or outcomes in the EHR.

◆

"What is the risk of recurrence after restarting a treatment?"

◆

"Among patients with a specific condition, how often is one treatment used compared with another?"

◆

"What outcomes occur after a medication, procedure, or diagnosis?"

◆

"How common is a clinical event in patients with certain characteristics?"

Clinical examples

These examples show the kind of real-world evidence CCES returns — interpretable summaries grounded in aggregate EHR data across multiple health systems. They are illustrative of what the service can provide; limitations are explicit in every response.

Example 1 · Diabetes & Peripheral Artery Disease
SGLT2i / GLP-1RA vs. Other Antidiabetics — Lower Limb Amputations
"Among patients with diabetes and peripheral artery disease, are patients treated with an SGLT2 inhibitor or GLP-1 receptor agonist less likely to have lower limb amputation compared with patients on other diabetes medications?"
17
Sites
67K
SGLT2/GLP-1 patients
0.66
Crude IRR

The observed lower limb amputation rate was approximately 55 per 1,000 in the SGLT2/GLP-1 group, compared with approximately 80 per 1,000 in the other diabetes medication group — a crude incidence rate ratio of 0.66, suggesting a lower observed incidence of amputation in the SGLT2/GLP-1 group.

Note: CCES does not make prescribing recommendations. This is aggregate real-world evidence to help inform clinical thinking and patient discussion. Limitations: this analysis did not fully account for baseline differences, comorbidities, treatment duration, dose, or patients who may have used medications from both groups.
Example 2 · Pulmonology / Infectious Disease
ICS vs. Non-ICS — Candida Esophagitis Recurrence
"Among patients who previously had Candida esophagitis and were on inhaled corticosteroids, are they more likely to have recurrence if restarted on ICS compared with similar patients who are not restarted?"
575
ICS patients
465
Non-ICS patients
1.40
Crude IRR

Patients who resumed inhaled corticosteroids had approximately 40% higher observed incidence of recurrent Candida esophagitis — recurrence rates of 191 vs. 129 per 1,000 patients. The crude incidence rate ratio was 1.40.

Note: this is not a treatment recommendation. It is real-world evidence to inform the clinical conversation. Limitations include possible confounding and the fact that the benefit of the inhaled steroid is not measured. Candida esophagitis: fungal infection of the esophagus caused by overgrowth of Candida yeast.
