Dr. Kravchenko is the primary research faculty of PITTCARE. Her work focuses on the practical application of artificial intelligence and machine learning in family medicine and primary care — with a consistent emphasis on translation: ensuring that methods developed in research reach real clinicians and real patients.

Her research spans three interconnected areas. First, developing practical AI/ML and statistical models that can be translated into clinical settings — particularly in the family medicine and primary care domains. Second, creating analytical tools that inform clinical decision support by leveraging electronic health records to extract patient-specific information and contextualize clinical alerts. Third, translational research using large-scale EHR datasets including the NCATS-funded National COVID Cohort Collaborative (N3C), the NIH-funded All of Us program, and the ENACT Network.

Her most recent interest is AI ethics — exploring data responsibility, algorithmic fairness, the misuse of technology, safe integration of AI into clinical practice, long-term monitoring, and accountability. This thread connects directly to PITTCARE's Voice of the People Survey: the question of who gets to shape how Intelligence-Technology enters the exam room.

Dr. Kravchenko holds a PhD in Chemistry from the University of British Columbia and completed an NLM postdoctoral fellowship at the Pitt Department of Biomedical Informatics — the same department that provides PITTCARE's foundational informatics partnership through Dr. Visweswaran.

Active project · ENACT Network
Concierge Clinical
Evidence Service (CCES)
A clinician-focused pilot service for answering real-world clinical questions with real-world EHR data
Submit a question ↗

Uses de-identified, aggregate EHR data from up to 50 participating health systems — giving answers grounded in real patient populations, not just published trial cohorts.

Supports questions where existing evidence or clinical guidelines are limited or unclear — the gap between what guidelines say and what the patient in front of you looks like.

Returns an interpretable summary to help inform clinical decision-making — written for clinicians, not data scientists, with explicit acknowledgment of limitations.

Free to clinicians and supported by grant funding — no cost, no contract, no informatics expertise required to submit a question.

How it works

01
Submit your question

Go to the CCES website and complete a short form with your contact information and clinical question.

02
Refine the evidence

The CCES team follows up to clarify your question and translates it into an EHR query across the ENACT network.

03
Analyze the data

The query runs against de-identified, aggregate data from up to 50 participating health systems. No informatics expertise required.

04
Receive a concise response

You receive an interpretable summary — real-world evidence to inform your thinking and your conversation with the patient.

Questions CCES can answer

Best suited for questions that can be defined using diagnoses, medications, procedures, laboratory results, encounters, or outcomes in the EHR.

◆

"What is the risk of recurrence after restarting a treatment?"

◆

"Among patients with a specific condition, how often is one treatment used compared with another?"

◆

"What outcomes occur after a medication, procedure, or diagnosis?"

◆

"How common is a clinical event in patients with certain characteristics?"

Active project · Qualitative Study
Ambient Intelligence in Healthcare
Exploring its impact on patient-provider interactions and health equity through patient and provider perspectives

About the study

Ambient Intelligence (AmI) tools listen to clinical conversations in real time — transcribing, summarizing, and generating after-visit notes — without requiring the clinician to type. They are entering family medicine practices rapidly. But whether they help or hinder the patient-provider relationship, and whether they perform equitably across diverse patient populations, is largely unknown.

This study fills that gap through a qualitative mixed-methods design: focus groups with family medicine providers and in-depth one-on-one interviews with patients — drawn from a diverse range of practices across the Pittsburgh region, including Altoona, Squirrel Hill, Shadyside, St. Margaret's, Shenango Valley, McKeesport, and Williamsburg.

Why it matters for PITTCARE

Ambient AI is one of the fastest-moving categories of Intelligence-Technology entering primary care — and one of the least studied from the patient perspective. This study directly enacts PITTCARE's commitment to co-producing knowledge with the people most affected by the technologies we study.

The findings will provide actionable recommendations for how ambient AI tools should be designed, implemented, and governed in family medicine — grounded in the real experiences of patients and providers, not just technical benchmarks.

What the study investigates

Provider focus groups

◆

Does ambient AI streamline documentation or create new complications — and how does this affect burnout?

◆

How do AmI tools affect collaboration with nurses, medical assistants, and the broader care team?

◆

Do auto-generated summaries align with patient concerns and remain patient-centered in clinical decision-making?

◆

How do these tools perform across diverse patient populations — and do they introduce or alleviate bias in documentation?

Patient interviews

◆

Were patients informed about ambient AI use — and how does knowing (or not knowing) affect their trust in their provider?

◆

Do after-visit summaries actually help patients understand their care — across varying literacy levels, languages, and disabilities?

◆

Do certain groups — by race, age, or socioeconomic status — face disadvantages when ambient AI is present in their care?

◆

How do patients feel about having their clinical conversations transcribed — and what would make this feel safer and more respectful?

Methods at a glance

3
Provider focus groups

5–8 family medicine providers per group; 60–90-minute virtual sessions via Microsoft Teams. Purposive sampling across diverse Pittsburgh-area practices.

20
Patient interviews

60-minute one-on-one interviews, virtual or telephone. Participants reflect diversity in age, gender, race, and socioeconomic status.

◎
Thematic analysis

Dual-coded transcripts in NVivo with saturation tracking, Cohen's kappa intercoder reliability (target ≥ 0.71), and COREQ-compliant reporting.

◆
Actionable outputs

Recommendations for ambient AI design, implementation, and governance in family medicine — grounded in patient and provider experience.

Collaborator spotlight · Independent prior work · JAMIA 2026

Multi-Agent LLMs for Secondary Headache Diagnosis

A collaboration between Dr. Shyam Visweswaran and Dr. Yanshan Wang, published in the Journal of the American Medical Informatics Association, applying multi-agent large language models to a high-stakes primary care diagnostic challenge.

This work was conducted independently and predates PITTCARE — the center claims no credit for it. It's included here because Dr. Wang and Dr. Visweswaran are PITTCARE collaborators, and their track record on projects like this one is representative of the caliber of work happening across the partnerships the center is built on.

Most headaches seen in primary care are benign — but a small fraction signal a dangerous underlying cause: aneurysm, arteritis, mass, or infection. Distinguishing "secondary" headache from routine presentations under time pressure is exactly the kind of diagnostic reasoning task where clinical decision support could help, if it can be trusted.

The team built a multi-agent system: an orchestrator agent coordinates seven specialist agents, each screening for one red-flag pattern — thunderclap onset, meningismus, papilledema, temporal arteritis, systemic illness, focal neurologic deficits, and first-or-worst headache. Evaluated on 90 expert-validated cases, the multi-agent approach using structured prompting (GPrompt) achieved the best performance of any configuration tested: F1 = 0.605 with the Qwen-30B model.

Pitt Med Year 2 · Instructor: John S. Maier, PhD, MD

2

About the course

Taught by Dr. Maier to second-year students at the University of Pittsburgh School of Medicine, this course places critical appraisal — the foundational skill of evidence-based medicine — directly in the context of artificial intelligence. Students learn to evaluate AI tools not as passive users but as informed clinicians: asking whether the evidence supports a tool's use, how it performs across populations, what its limitations are, and when it should and should not guide clinical decisions.

All enrolled students are required to complete the first two modules of the STFM Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning for Primary Care (AiM-PC) curriculum — the national curriculum developed by the Society of Teachers of Family Medicine specifically for training clinicians in the responsible use of AI.

Why this matters for PITTCARE

This course directly addresses the workforce development dimension of PITTCARE's mission — and it does so at the earliest stage of a physician's training. Medical students who learn to critically appraise AI tools as second-years will carry that framework through residency, into practice, and into the patient encounters where the real work happens.

The required STFM AiM-PC module integration means that Pitt's second-year medical students are now connected to the same national AI curriculum that anchors the STFM Centers of Excellence recognition program — making this course a direct contribution to PITTCARE's CoE readiness in the Education and Workforce domain.

What the course builds

Critical appraisal skills

Students learn to evaluate AI tools using the same evidence-based framework they apply to clinical literature — asking about validation, generalizability, population performance, and limitations before trusting a tool with patient care.

National curriculum integration

Required completion of STFM AiM-PC Modules 1 and 2 ensures every student receives the nationally developed foundational AI curriculum for primary care clinicians — grounding Pitt training in a shared national standard.

Early workforce development

By teaching critical appraisal of AI at Year 2, the course reaches physicians before they form their clinical habits — building a generation of primary care clinicians who approach Intelligence-Technology with both skill and appropriate skepticism.

FM PittNet

A joint Clinical and Translational Science Institute (CTSI) and Department of Family Medicine Practice-Based Research Network (PBRN), established in 2023 to hasten the translation of research into practice.

FM PittNet exists to close the distance between research and the exam room — connecting primary care patients, practices, providers, and investigators in a standing collaborative network built for practical, practice-based research.

For PITTCARE, FM PittNet is the real-world clinical pipeline: the network through which Intelligence-Technology research developed with DBMI, SHRS, and SCI can be tested, refined, and validated in actual primary care practices — not just in retrospective data or simulated settings.

Network details

Joint initiative of the Clinical and Translational Science Institute (CTSI) and the Department of Family Medicine. A Practice-Based Research Network (PBRN) connecting primary care patients, practices, providers, and investigators toward faster translation of research into practice.
